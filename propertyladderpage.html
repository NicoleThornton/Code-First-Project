What is a Mortgage?

A mortgage is a type of loan that is taken out with the specific purpose of buying property or land. Most last for the duration of 25 years but the term of the mortgage can be longer or shorter, depending on the type of mortgage you get. Should you be unable to make you repayments, the lender can repossess your home and sell it to get their money back.

For this reason, it is crucial that you think about whether you can truly afford to keep up with your monthly repayments. You should also take into consideration the costs of owning a home, including household bills, council tax, insurance and maintenance. As a result, lenders will want to see proof of your income and expenditure, and will also consider if you have any debts. This is to reassure the lender that should interest rates rise, you will be able to keep up repayments. They have the right to refuse to offer you a mortgage if they do not think that you can afford it.

Finding A Mortgage

You can apply for a mortgage, either directly from a bank or building society and can choose from their product range. There is a spectrum of different mortgage deals available. It is very important that you find the right one for you. With so much selection, this can be a difficult process, as it depends on various factors. If this is the case, you can turn to a mortgage broker or independent financial adviser who will compare different mortgages on the market for you. There are two main types of mortgages, including Fixed Rate and Variable Rate mortgages.

Fixed Rate Mortgages:

If you choose this option, the interest rate paid will remain the same throughout the length of the mortgage irrespective of whether interest rates change due to economic factors.

Variable Rate Mortgages:

With this option, the interest rates are subject to economic change and therefore, can change at any given time. As a result, it is important that you have some savings available, should the price of your mortgage increase.

Type of Mortgage: Advantages: Disadvantages:
Fixed Rate Mortgages
  • Monthly payments remain the same, helping you budget long-term
  • Usually higher than variable rate mortgages to compensate for possible changes in interest rates
  • Will not benefit from decrease in interest rates
Variable Rate Mortgages
  • Generally cheaper than Fixed Rate Mortgages
  • Can benefit from paying less should interest rates decline
  • Interest rates subject to volatile economy and changing interest rates
  • Harder to budget long term as the amount of payments can vary

There are different types of Variable Rate Mortgages (VRM), as described in the table below:

Type of VRM: Description Advantages: Disadvantages:
Standard Variable Rate This is the normal interest rate your mortgage lender charges homebuyers. It will last as long as your mortgage or until you take out another mortgage deal.
  • Freedom. You can overpay or leave your mortgage at any given time
  • Changes to the interest rate can occur after a rise or fall in the base rate set by the Bank of England
Discount Mortgages This provides a discount off the lender’s Standard Variable Rate. It only applies for a certain length of time, usually for two or three years.
  • Rates begin cheaper so that monthly repayments are lower
  • If the lender you choose cuts its Standard variable Rate, you will pay less monthly
  • The lender can raise its Standard Variable Rate at any time, making it really important to budget in case this happens
  • If the base rate rises, it is likely that the discount rate will increase too
Tracker Mortgages Tracker mortgages move directly in line with another interest rates, (usually the Bank of England’s base rate + a few percent).
  • If the tracking rate falls, the price of your mortgage repayments will do the same
  • If the tracking rate increases, your mortgage will do the same
  • May be subject to an early repayment charge if you decide to switch mortgages before the deal ends
Capped Rate Mortgages Your interest rate moves according to the lender’s SVR. However, this will be capped to a certain point so the rate will not increase beyond a certain level.  This gives you certainty that your rate will not rise beyond a certain level
  • Mortgage is cheaper should the SVR fall
  • Usually higher than other variable and fixed rates
  • Your lender has the autonomy to change the rate at any time up to the level of the cap

Applying for a Mortgage

Stage 1:

This involves a basic fact find to determine how much you can afford and what type of mortgage is suitable for you. This will include:

  • Asking series of questions to determine what type of mortgage you want and how long you want it for
  • Finding out your financial situation to give an indication of how much a lender might be prepared to lend you
  • They will give you all the key information you need about the mortgage, their service and any fees or charges if applicable

Stage 2:

This is when the mortgage lender will conduct a much more thorough affordability check and evidence of income.

  • This is when you will begin your application
  • The lender/mortgage broker will begin a full ‘fact find’ and thorough affordability assessment
  • You will need to provide evidence of your income and expenditure
  • You may be asked questions about your finances and future plans that can impact your future income

Your Deposit

When purchasing a property, you will have to pay a deposit which goes towards the cost of the property that you are buying. The more deposit you pay the lower your interest rates can be. A deposit for your desired property will cost approximately 5% to 20% of its total value.
